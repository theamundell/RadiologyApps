Introduction

The following 12 cases are presented almost in their entirety,
this includes the provided clinical information at the time of
each study. Most have been performed as a standard MRI
spondylitis protocol.

Advise reviewing the imaging first and reach a diagnosis.
Then reveal the clinical information to assess its impact on
your diagnosis and whether you change your diagnosis.

Finally reveal the imaging report!

  • Images are available as a play through video and looped twice
  • If you are new to imaging of SpA you can review the MRI Spondyloarthropathy Checklist.

MRI Spondyloarthropathy Checklist

  • It is important that you have a standardized approach to reviewing an imaging study.
  • Confirm patient name, study date.
  • Review the provided clinical information (particular attention to age, ASAS criteria, history of prior truma/infection etc)
  • Check for prior imaging studies including X-rays, MRI, CT, Bone scan. Note that studies do not need to be dedicated to the current clinical question but may have included the relevant anatomy, e.g. CT abdomen and pelvis for a prior appendicitis will include the lumbar spine, pelvis and hip joints.
  • Ensure all the required sequences have been acquired and are of diagnostic quality e.g. no image degradation due to motion or incomplete studies due to patient claustrophobia
  • Review localizer images (may reveal occult pathology)
  • You can start with the coronal oblique T1 and STIR images of the sacroiliac joint. This is my preferred starting point as this will often reveal the diagnosis given the clinical question of SpA.

MRI Spondyloarthropathy Checklist

Sacroiliac Joints

  • Joint space: Normal, widened, narrow, cartilage loss, joint effusion/synovitis.
  • Subchondral bone plate: Normal, irregularity, erosions, subchondral cysts.
  • Subchondral marrow: Normal, PIFA (Post Inflammatory Fat Accentuation), sclerosis, osteitis.
  • Capsule/Ligaments/Tendons: Normal, capsulitis, enthesitis
  • I then review the spine

MRI Spondyloarthropathy Checklist

Spine

  • Assess Anterior and Posterior Elements
  • Vertebrae: Count vertebrae (important for identification transitional vertebra), vertebral body height, alignment, Pars Interarticularis (for pars defects, usually at L4,L5).
  • Marrow: Normal, PIFA (Post Inflammatory Fat Accentuation), sclerosis, osteitis
  • Joints (Facet, Costovertebral, Costotransverse, Sternoclavicular, Atlantoaxial): Normal, osteitis, effusion, capsulitis, degenerative, erosions, fused.
  • Soft tissues: Normal, Enthesitis
  • Intervertebral disc: Normal, degenerative, spondylodisciitis (infective and non-infective)
  • Review craniovertebral junction, spinal canal and cord.

MRI Spondyloarthropathy Checklist

The final diagnosis is going to be a combination of clinical and imaging findings, i.e. imaging findings should be assessed in the correct clinical context. If there is a discrepancy the case should be discussed with the referring clinican.

Case 1

Clinical Indication: Extensive spine tenderness, 2 year duration of inflammatory back pain.

  • Multiplanar imaging sequences acquired as per spondyloarthropathy
  • Protocol: Sagittal T1, STIR whole spine and Coronal T1, STIR sacroiliac joints
  • No prior study for correlation.
  • Sacroiliac joint spaces are maintained, no joint effusion, no subchondral bone marrow edema, no post inflammatory fat accentuation nor erosive disease identified. Minimal cortical irregularity anteriorly. No features to suggest capsulitis. Normal signal intensity entheseal compartment.
  • Vertebral body height, alignment is normal. Normal vertebral marrow signal intensity with no focal osteitis. No postinflammatory fat accentuation lesions. No syndesmophyte formation. Posterior paraspinal soft tissues are normal. No facet joint effusions.
  • No significant disc disease identified. Spinal cord ends at the upper border of T12. Normal craniocervical junction.
  • Impression.
  • Essentially normal study as described. Correlation with HLA-B27, inflammatory markers. In a small percentage of patient's with definite clinical findings imaging studies may be negative.

Case 2

Clinical Indication: History of right sacroiliac joint infection and periarticular abscess 18 months previous, successfully treated. Lower inflammatory back pain, ? underlying SpA versus symptoms related to prior infection.

Comparison

Comparison is made with the series of MR examinations of the spine and sacroiliac joints and bone scan 18 months previously, prior study demonstrated right paraspinal abscess with secondary right sacroiliac joint infection.

Sacroiliac Joints

Right-sided erosions at the sacroiliac joint with associated subchondral sclerosis, widening of the joint space, and a small joint effusion. There is no bony ankylosis and the left sacroiliac joint is normal in appearance. Appearances are in keeping with resolving infection. No residual soft tissue or epidural collection is demonstrated. Please note that dedicated axial images have not been performed and the paraspinal muscles have not been fully assessed.

Spine

No structural or inflammatory changes within the examined spine to suggest a diagnosis of seronegative spondyloarthropathy. There is reversal of the normal cervical lordosis with moderate disc osteophyte complexes of the mid and lower cervical spine that indent the anterior thecal sac. There is no myelomalacia demonstrated. If there is clinical concern a dedicated MR of the cervical spine is advised for full assessment.

There is a focus of intermediate T1 and increased T2 signal intensity within the right L1 transverse process that is felt likely to represent an incidental hemangioma.

The cord is normal in appearance and terminates at L1. The cauda equina is unremarkable and the craniocervical junction is normal in appearance.

Impression

Right sacroiliac joint post-infective sacroillitis as described. No MR evidence to support a diagnosis of a seronegative spondyloarthropathy.

Moderate disc osteophyte complexes of the mid and lower cervical spine as described requiring clinical correlation.
