Radiography

  • Radiography is the backbone of imaging with many imaging algorithms starting with an X-Ray.
  • The hand radiograph is one of the most valuable imaging tools in Rheumatology.

Radiography has many inherent advantages:

  • provides excellent resolution of bony architecture
  • has limited radiation exposure
  • multiple regions can be imaged in a short time interval, e.g. bilateral hands and feet
  • it is readily accessible and is low cost
  • viewing stations allow for easy side to side comparisons of X-Rays over multiple time points
  • physicians in general are more comfortable reviewing radiographs than more complex cross-sectional imaging studies such as MRI
  • radiography has the longest research history in the progression of imaging changes in arthritis than any other imaging modality

There are some disadvantages which include:

  • inability to directly assess active inflammation and early disease (osteitis, synovitis, tenosynovitis etc) as can be assessed on MRI
  • less sensitive for detecting early erosive disease when compared to CT, MRI and ultrasound

In evaluation of the arthritides, the two most important radiographic projections of the hands are:

  1. Posterior-Anterior (PA)
  2. Anterior-Posterior Oblique (“Ball-Catcher’s”)
  3. Other radiographic projections include:
    • Lateral projection - used mainly in trauma and is of less importance in rheumatology
    • PA Oblique

Posterior-Anterior Projection (PA)

  • Radiograph is shot from above with palms of hands flat on cassette.
  • Include entire hand and distal 2-3cm of the radius
  • Centered to the third MCPJ (metacarpophalangeal joint)
  • Excellent for assessment of joint spaces, bone density, soft tissue swelling and non-reducible subluxation/deformities.

Anterior-Posterior Oblique projection (“Ball-Catchers”)

  • Hands are obliqued 45 degrees, AP projection and centred between the hands at the level of the MCPJs
  • This projection is optimal for seeing early erosions at the MCP joints, especially at radial margins of 2nd to 5th MCPJs, pisotriquetral joint and the 3-5th carpo-metacarpal joints.

Wrist Radiographs

  • The wrist may be imaged separately from the hand.
  • Usually performed for localized pathology including fractures, infection, SLAC wrist etc.
  • Views include:

    • PA
    • PA Internal Oblique
    • PA External Oblique
    • Lateral
    • Carpal Tunnel
    • Scaphoid views

Tomosynthesis

  • This is similar to conventional tomography. However in tomosynthesis only one sweep of the x-ray tube is made during which multiple exposures are taken which can be reconstructed to produce multiple images at different levels
  • The x-ray tube follows a small rotation angle of <40°. The thickness of each slice can be changed depending on the anatomy of the structure being investigated.
  • There has seen a resurgence of interest in tomosynthesis during the last decade with the availability of flat panel radiographic detectors that would allow low noise rapid image acquisition without geometric distortion.
  • Recent research has demonstrated increased sensitivity and specificity for erosive disease in the hands and wrists .
  • The radiation exposure is low, comparing 30 x1mm slices with 2 standard radiographs of the hands, the radiation dose is less than 3 times that of standard radiographs.
  • Tomosynthesis will likely play a significant role in the future in the detection of erosive disease in rheumatology.

Conventional Tomography

  • This has gained a lower position on the imaging options with the advent of cross-sectional imaging techniques such as CT and MRI.
  • The X-ray source and detector are moved in a set path during an exposure. Anatomy at a set depth remains in focus while structures at superficial and deeper levels are blurred.
  • It is rarely used in the radiology department
