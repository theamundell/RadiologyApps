Case 2.6
Review the following case and provide a diagnosis

Clinical Presentation

History
  • 76 year old man
  • History of hypertension and chronic kidney disease with stable baseline creatinine of 120
  • Pain and swelling of the feet, particularly the toes, and later the ankles - initially episodic, but now persistent pain in the feet
  • Over the past year, pain and swelling off and on in the hands or wrists
  • Morning stiffness of 2 hours
  • Review of systems and social history non-contributory
Physical Examination
  • Appears well
  • Temp 37.2, HR 60, bp 162/90
  • Swelling, warmth, and mild erythema of scattered MCP’s, PIP’s, and DIP’s - 5 swollen joints in the hands
  • Tenderness of all MTP’s and midfoot bilaterally.
  • Swelling, and warmth of left ankle
  • No other swollen or tender joints
  • Firm subcutaneous soft tissue nodules surrounding the DIP’s of several digits, abutting 1st MTP’s, and scattered over toes
  • Remainder of the examination unremarkable
Labs
  • Complete Blood Count - normal
  • Creatinine 122 (at baseline)
  • Alanine Aminotransferase 18, Alkaline Phosphatase 114, Albumin 40
  • Rheumatoid Factor negative, Cyclic Citrullinated Peptide negative
  • Antinuclear Antibody negative
  • Hepatitis serology negative
  • ESR 20, CRP 10.2
Synovial Fluid Analysis
  • The attending physician was not confident that aspirate could be obtained
  • Radiologic investigations ordered

Differential Diagnosis - What diagnoses are you considering?

The differential for a polyarthritis involving hands and feet includes:
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Psoriatic arthritis
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Crystalline arthropathies ie. CPPD, gout
  • Infection-associated, eg. hepatitis C arthropathy
  • Connective tissue diseases, eg. SLE

Imaging - What studies would you order?

Radiographs
Radiograph (AP, bilateral feet)
Describe what you see
  • Pertinent abnormalities are multifocal soft tissue densities overlying erosions
  • Erosions are periarticular, and ‘punched-out’ in appearance
  • Some erosions have sclerotic margins
  • Joint space is preserved
  • No periarticular osteopenia
Radiograph (AP, right hand)
Describe what you see
  • Arrows denote erosions with “overhanging edges”

What is the Diagnosis?

The most likely diagnosis is tophaceous gout.

This is the most likely diagnosis, given:

  • Typical history with progressive polyarticular presentation, initially episodic, then becoming chronic
  • Risk factors for gout including hypertension and chronic kidney disease
  • Soft tissue nodules of toes and fingers, with underlying erosions seen on x-ray
  • Typical findings on x-ray
Other Imaging Modalities
Ultrasound

Pertinent abnormalities include:

  • Joint distension
  • Hyperechoic foci within and underlying cortical irregularity
MRI

Pertinent abnormalities include:

  • Joint effusion
  • Active synovitis
  • Pre-erosive change medial margin of the metatarsal head (arrow)
