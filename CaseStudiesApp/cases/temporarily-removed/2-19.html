Case 2.19
Review the following case and provide a diagnosis

Clinical Presentation

History
  • 55 year old woman
  • No other medical history
  • Several years of pain and swelling affecting the wrists, MCP’s, PIP’s, DIP’s
  • Gradually progressive and is beginning to interfere with her activiities
  • Morning stiffness of 45 minutes
Physical Examination
  • Appears well
  • Temp 36.5, HR 80, bp 124/80
  • Swelling of both wrists, and several MCP’s, PIP’s, and DIP’s
  • Range of motion of both wrists limited to 45 degrees of flexion and 20 degrees of extension
  • Bouchard’s and Heberden’s nodes present on PIP’s and DIP’s
  • No tophi were found
  • Remainder of the examination unremarkable

Differential Diagnosis - What diagnoses are you considering?

  • The differential for a polyarthritis involving hands and wrists includes:
    • Psoriatic arthritis
    • Crystalline arthropathies ie. CPPD, gout
    • Rheumatoid arthritis
    • Osteoarthritis
    • Infection-associated, eg. hepatitis C arthropathy
    • Connective tissue diseases, eg. SLE

Imaging - What studies would you order?

Radiographs
Radiograph (AP bilateral hands)
Describe what you see

On this radiograph, note:

  • Chondrocalcinosis in the triangular fibrocartilagenous complex (TFCC)
  • Degenerative changes in radiocarpal joints
  • Scapholunate advanced collapse (SLAC) deformity, with widened scapholunate interval, scaphoid rotation, proximal capitate migration and degenerative changes
  • MCP joint space loss
  • Some osteophytic lipping at MCP’s
  • Lack of periarticular osteopenia
  • Lack of erosions at MCP’s
Radiograph (magnified MCP)
Describe what you see

On this radiograph, note:

  • Capsular and intra-articular calcification
  • Joint space loss
  • Large subchondral cyst of the proximal phalanx
  • Mild soft tissue swelling
Ultrasound
  • Ultrasound was performed to aid in diagnostic joint aspirate
Ultrasound (2nd MCP, longitudinal)
Describe what you see
  • Note hyperechoic crystals within the joint (arrow)

What is the Diagnosis?

The most likely diagnosis is the chronic presentation of calcium pyrophosphate deposition disease (CPPD) (occasionally termed “pseudorheumatoid”)

This is the most likely diagnosis, given:

  • Typical joint distribution with wrist, and MCP involvement
  • Degenerative changes on examination
  • Typical findings on radiographs with chondrocalcinosis, and degenerative changes in atypical joints such as MCP’s and wrists
  • Crystals visible on U/S of MCP
