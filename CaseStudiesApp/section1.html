Case 1.1
Review the following images and provide a diagnosis

Diagnosis: Achilles’ tendinosis secondary to erosive arthropathy

  • The posterosuperior aspect of the calcaneus demonstrates extensive erosions with some new bone formation (arrows) at the Achilles’ entheseal complex.
  • The distal Achilles’ tendon is thickened (line).
  • There is normal bone density.
  • There is no new bone formation in the region of the plantar spur or soft tissue thickening in the region of the retrocalcaneal bursa

Features are in keeping with an erosive arthropathy, more likely a seronegative such as psoriatic arthropathy given the new bone formation. Rheumatoid would look similar except for the new bone formation.

Reference

  • Ref: Chapter 5: Inflammatory Arthritides; O'Neill, JMD, Essential Imaging in Rheumatology; New York: Springer Science+Business Media; 2015

Case 1.4
Review the following images and provide a diagnosis

Diagnosis: Chronic Sacroiliitis

  • T1-weighted (left) and T2-fat saturated (FS) (right) - low signal intensity on iliac side of the SI joint bilaterally on T1 and T2 in keeping with sclerosis (arrow).
  • High signal intensity on T1 and low on T2 FS on sacral side represents post-inflammatory fat accentuation (line).
  • There is subtle cortical irregularity of the left SI joint on the iliac aspect suggesting early erosions (best seen on T1).
  • High signal intensity of the left SI joint on T2 may represent effusion or mild synovitis (gadolinium enhancement would help to distinguish, but this is rarely required in clinical practice)

Case 1.5
Review the following images of this 87-year old woman with foot pain and provide a diagnosis

Diagnosis: Tophaceous gout

  • There are multifocal periarticular and extra-articular erosions (arrows) at joint margins affecting MTP’s, PIP’s, tarsometatarsal joints with overlying soft tissue densities indicating tophi
  • There is digital resorption secondary to tophi at the right and left 4th phalanges
  • There is vascular calcification (first intermetatarsal spaces bilaterally) indicative of metabolic risk factors associated with gout, such as renal disease
  • Osteopenia particularly of the first metatarsal

References

  • Ref: Chapter 8: Crystal Disease; O'Neill, JMD, Essential Imaging in Rheumatology; New York: Springer Science+Business Media; 2015

Case 1.6
Review the following images

What is the most likely cause of this 18-year old man’s shoulder pain?

  • a) Osteoarthritis
  • b) Juvenile idiopathic arthritis
  • c) Hydroxyapatite deposition disease
  • d) Septic arthritis

Diagnosis: Juvenile idiopathic arthritis

  • The most likely diagnosis is b) juvenile idiopathic arthritis, given the large marginal erosion adjacent to the head of the humerus.
  • There are no significant degenerative changes (such as osteophytosis, joint space loss and subchondral sclerosis/cysts), soft tissue calcifications, nor osteolysis of the humeral head to suggest HADD.
  • There is no periarticular osteopenia, capsular distension or erosions without sclerotic margin (usually indicates a pressure erosion or inactive erosive arthropathy)

References

  • Ref: Chapter 7: Inflammatory Arthritis; O'Neill, JMD, Essential Imaging in Rheumatology; New York: Springer Science+Business Media; 2015

Case 1.7
Review the following images and provide a diagnosis

Diagnosis: Paget’s disease with pathological fracture

  • Paget’s disease is a condition of increased osteoclast activity
    • Phase 1: osteolytic phase with bone resorption
    • Phase 2: excessive new bone formation out of keeping the degree of available substrate, leading the production of structurally inferior woven bone predisposed to fracture
    • Phase 3: Inactive phase, with development of calcified, sclerotic bone
  • In this radiograph, coarsening and thickening of mid and distal humeral cortex and trabeculae is seen with areas of subchondral lucency. Note the distal humeral expansion, which is a helpful sign in the diagnosis of Paget’s
  • The fracture has occurred due to the presence of structurally inferior bone

References

  • Ref: Chapter 9: Endocrine and Miscellaneous Arthropathies; O'Neill, JMD, Essential Imaging in Rheumatology; New York: Springer Science+Business Media; 2015

Case 1.10
Review the following images. What is the diagnosis?

Diagnosis: Rheumatoid Arthritis

AP Shoulder Radiograph

  • Advanced glenohumeral degeneration with complete joint space loss, subchondral sclerosis and cyst formation.
  • No definite erosions.
  • Cortical changes of the greater tuberosity.
  • The acromiohumeral interval is maintained. There is soft tissue prominence over the greater tuberosity suggesting significantly distended subacromial-subdeltoid bursa (arrows).

Longitudinal Shoulder Ultrasound

  • Rice bodies (arrows) in the distended subacromial-subdeltoid bursa which also demonstrated synovial proliferation with internal flow on Doppler (not shown).
  • “Supraspin" refers to the supraspinatus tendon deep to the bursa but not clearly demonstrated on this image
  • There is no evidence of trauma.
  • The acromiohumeral interval is normal making rotator cuff arthropathy unlikely.
  • No soft tissue calcification or chondrocalcinosis to suggest crystal disease although this remains within the differential.
  • The ultrasound finding of rice bodies suggests Rheumatoid Arthritis

References

  • Ref: Chapter 5: Inflammatory Arthritides; O'Neill, JMD, Essential Imaging in Rheumatology; New York: Springer Science+Business Media; 2015

Case 1.15
Review the following images. What is the diagnosis?

Diagnosis: Diffuse Idiopathic Skeletal Hyperostosis (DISH)

  • Lateral cervical spine radiograph demonstrating chronic new bone formation along the anterior vertebral bodies (ossification of the anterior longitudinal ligament).
  • The ossification has a “flowing” appearance, and extends over 4 contiguous vertebral bodies (this is required for a diagnosis of DISH)
  • Note the break in ossification at C3-4 with corticated margins. This may be related to a prior fracture of the ossified ligament

Case 1.17
Provide the most likely diagnosis for this 52M with persistent left leg and ankle pain and swelling post-femoral fracture, based on the following radiographs.

Diagnosis

  • The most likely diagnosis is complex regional pain syndrome, otherwise known as Sudeck’s atrophy, given the following findings:
    • Generalized soft tissue swelling of the entire distal limb
    • Severe generalized osteopenia
    • Consistent history with prior injury to the affected limb

    References

    • Ref: Chapter 11: Metabolic Bone Disease; O'Neill, JMD, Essential Imaging in Rheumatology; New York: Springer Science+Business Media; 2015
